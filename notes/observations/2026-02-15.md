# Observations — 2026-02-15

## "Capacity Ambiguity" — larger models show more mid-layer uncertainty

**Prompt:** "ARIA stands for Accessible Rich Internet" → target: " Applications"

**Observation:** GPT-2 XL briefly entertains "Protocol" as a completion
(layers 18, 21, 23) — a candidate that neither Medium nor Large ever
considered. Despite having more capacity and ultimately higher confidence
(60.3% vs 45.8% vs 2.7%), XL spends more layers oscillating between
plausible completions before resolving.

**Logit lens top predictions across models:**

- **Medium (24L):** Explorer → sites → Services → Access (never reaches Applications)
- **Large (36L):** Explorer (layers 4-21) → Access → Applications (layer 32)
- **XL (48L):** Explorer (layers 0-17) → Explorer/Protocol/access oscillation (18-34) → Applications (layer 35)

**Hypothesis:** Larger models encode more plausible associations for a given
context, which creates genuine ambiguity in the middle layers. The model
"knows more" and therefore has more competing hypotheses to resolve before
the correct answer wins. The extra capacity doesn't make mid-layer processing
more confident — it makes it *less* confident, with resolution pushed to
later layers where MLP capacity can overpower the alternatives.

This would predict: for well-known facts (e.g., "The capital of France is"),
larger models should resolve *earlier* because the correct answer dominates
so thoroughly. The ambiguity effect should be strongest for domain-specific
or moderately obscure knowledge where multiple plausible completions exist.

**Needs:** More prompts across knowledge domains and obscurity levels to
confirm. Specifically want to compare: (1) a slam-dunk fact, (2) a
moderately known domain term, (3) a niche domain term, across all three
model sizes.

## MLP dominance increases with scale for ARIA

**Prompt:** Same ARIA prompt across GPT-2 Medium / Large / XL.

**Observation:** The mechanism for completing "ARIA → Applications" shifts
dramatically from attention-led to MLP-led as models scale:

| Model   | Correct? | Final prob | Mechanism              | Peak attention | Peak MLP  |
|---------|----------|------------|------------------------|----------------|-----------|
| Medium  | No       | 2.7% (r3)  | Attention-led (L21)    | 21.09 (L21)    | 16.14 (L18) |
| Large   | Yes      | 45.8%      | Late MLP (L30-35)      | 6.24 (L21)     | 10.02 (L34) |
| XL      | Yes      | 60.3%      | Late MLP (L42-47)      | 4.24 (L33)     | 20.07 (L47) |

In Medium, L21H6 (the same head that drives Paris factual recall) is
drafted into service for ARIA alongside the accessibility-specific L21H7.
By Large, no single attention head dominates. By XL, attention is nearly
irrelevant — L47 MLP alone contributes 20.07 logits with attention at -0.009.

**Interpretation:** Consistent with Geva et al. (2021) — MLPs act as
key-value memory stores. More MLP capacity → more facts stored directly →
less pressure on attention heads to route or compensate. Medium's attention
heads aren't doing something *better* — they're compensating for what the
MLPs can't store.

**Connection to WCAG results:** WCAG showed a similar pattern in Medium
where L21H7 pushed +45 logits for "Web" but the model still failed. The
MLP infrastructure wasn't there to support the signal. ARIA in Medium is
the intermediate case — attention finds the signal but can't fully deliver.
ARIA in Large/XL is the success case where MLPs carry the load.

This suggests a spectrum of knowledge accessibility:
- **Well-supported:** MLPs store it, attention routes it, model succeeds (Paris, ARIA in Large/XL)
- **Partially encoded:** Attention finds signal but MLPs can't back it up (ARIA in Medium, WCAG in Medium)
- **Not encoded:** Neither attention nor MLPs have it
